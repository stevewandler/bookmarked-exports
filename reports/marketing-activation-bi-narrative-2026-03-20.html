The Core Finding

There is a massive depth gap between what Bookmarked internally knows about Book Intelligence (as articulated in these four documents) and what the market sees. The current marketing describes BI at surface level — "understands books" and "compliance platform." The source documents reveal a sophisticated intelligence architecture with network effects, a trust model, ground truth verification, and a five-stage evolution from compliance to discovery. Almost none of this deeper story is being told externally.

This isn't a branding problem. The brand is strong. The messaging hierarchy is right. Library vitality first, compliance as supporting evidence — that's correct. What's missing is the depth of the intelligence story underneath that positioning. The market currently sees Bookmarked as a compliance tool that happens to use AI. The source documents reveal Bookmarked as an intelligence company that happens to enter through compliance. That distinction — tool vs. intelligence platform — is the messaging unlock this activation addresses.

  • Network Intelligence mentioned once — on /for-districts as "coming soon" card. Not on homepage or platform page.
  • Content Scanner mentioned — in About page and one FAQ ("will analyze what's actually inside the book").
Critical — Language Boundary Violations Found

The Playwright audit found language issues that contradict the BI narrative's hard boundaries:

  • "AI content analysis" — Platform page architecture diagram labels BI as "AI content analysis." This implies the AI reads book content, which it doesn't.
  • Oct 2025 blog post says BI "goes inside" books — Quote: "Our Book Intelligence, the 'Bookmarked Brain,' goes inside this secure layer to analyze the content." This is legacy investor-pitch language that directly contradicts the language boundary ("never imply we've read the book").
  • "AI-powered content profiles" (For Librarians page) — ambiguous; could imply profiles of book content rather than profiles about books.
  • Inconsistent naming: "Book Intelligence Shield" (homepage) vs "Book Intelligence" (platform) vs "Book Intelligence Profiles" (for-districts). Should be unified.
  • District count stale: Website shows 144 districts; internal docs say 150+.

Recommendation: These language issues should be fixed independent of the marketing activation. They create confusion about what BI does today and could expose Bookmarked to credibility risk if a customer or critic compares the website claims to the actual product.

Proposed Addition: The Intelligence Depth Section

A new section in messaging-consolidated.md under Brand Identity & Positioning that articulates what BI actually does, for use by all content-producing skills:

Proposed Section: "What Book Intelligence Actually Is"

Book Intelligence is the AI-powered engine underneath every surface in OnShelf. It operates across three layers: Layer 1 (Live Today): External Signals — everything publicly known about a book from metadata databases, ban/challenge lists, news coverage, professional reviews, and AI analysis of public information. Every piece of intelligence is sourced and traceable. Layer 2 (Building): Network Intelligence — intelligence derived from the collective activity of 150+ districts. Challenge velocity, placement patterns, decision benchmarks, review outcomes. Every district that uses OnShelf contributes to intelligence that makes the system smarter for every other district. Layer 3 (Future): Content Scanner — AI reads actual book content with publisher permission. Separate capability, separate timeline. For every book, BI produces an Intelligence Card: identity, content assessment, compliance signals, external flags, audience context, and advisory information. Think of it as "everything publicly known about a book, organized for decision-making." Key differentiator: Bookmarked is the only organization connected to actual library systems. When PEN America claims a book is banned, Bookmarked can check. This ground truth — verified from real library catalogs — is the highest trust tier in the system. Hard boundaries: BI operates on public information ABOUT books. It never reads the book itself (that's the Content Scanner). It never rates books. It never makes decisions — humans decide. It never replaces librarians — it gives them the data infrastructure they never had.
