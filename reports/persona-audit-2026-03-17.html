What's Strong

  • Superintendent persona is world-class — deep psychographics, objection frameworks, stakeholder maps, and real customer quotes across multiple systems
  • Librarian persona is equally strong with product-level (PRD) AND marketing-level detail, including a dedicated website messaging page
  • Psychographic buying segments (4 segments overlaid on personas) are a sophisticated layer most startups never build

  • Governance model is clear: hub files are CEO-locked, skills are read-only consumers

Critical Gaps

  • Parent/Guardian persona — has a full product PRD (4 epics, API endpoints) but NO marketing persona. OnShelf Engage is a product for parents with no marketing to parents.
  • Teacher persona — has a full product PRD (6 epics) but is excluded from ALL marketing persona work. The Classroom Library Module needs a buyer-journey for teacher adoption.
  • HubSpot has no persona property — contacts can't be segmented, campaigns can't be targeted, and reporting can't be filtered by persona. This is the operational gap that makes persona work theoretical instead of actionable.

Bottom Line Assessment

Your buyer personas (Superintendent, Librarian, Board Member, ESC Partner) are strong and actionable for sales conversations. Your user personas (Teacher, Parent) have deep product specs but zero marketing presence. The biggest structural issue isn't persona quality — it's that personas live in 3 disconnected layers (product PRDs, Confluence marketing pages, local psychographics file) with no single source of truth. And HubSpot can't operationalize any of it because there's no persona field.

Section 03

Assessment Framework

How we're grading each persona — and why these frameworks matter for an EdTech startup selling to school districts.

Revella's 5 Rings of Insight

Adele Revella's Buyer Personas framework defines 5 dimensions that separate useful personas from fiction. Each persona is scored on:

  • Priority Initiatives — What triggers them to act?
  • Success Factors — What does "good" look like to them?
  • Perceived Barriers — What stops them from buying?
  • Decision Criteria — How do they evaluate options?
  • Buyer's Journey — Where do they go for info? Who influences them?

Source: Buyer Personas by Adele Revella (Wiley, 2015). The gold standard for B2B persona methodology.

Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD)

Clayton Christensen's JTBD theory says people don't buy products — they "hire" them to do a job. Each persona is evaluated on 3 job layers:

  • Functional Job — The task they need done (e.g., "vet 200 book orders per semester")
  • Emotional Job — How they want to feel (e.g., "protected, not exposed")
  • Social Job — How they want to be perceived (e.g., "a responsible superintendent")

Source: Competing Against Luck by Clayton Christensen (HarperBusiness, 2016).

Why These Frameworks for Bookmarked

EdTech B2B sales to school districts have unique dynamics:

  • Multi-stakeholder buying committees — librarian champions, superintendent decision-makers, board ratifiers, CFO budget holders
  • Emotional + regulatory triggers — fear of liability, professional identity loss, legislative pressure
  • Long decision cycles with external catalysts — board meetings, parent complaints, ESC recommendations

Revella's 5 Rings capture the multi-stakeholder buying dynamics. JTBD captures the emotional depth that drives librarian advocacy and superintendent urgency. Together, they test whether personas are actually useful for content, sales, and product decisions.

Grading Scale

Grade Ring Score Meaning
A 9-10 per ring Exceptional. Research-validated, includes real customer language, actionable for content/sales/product decisions. Ready to operationalize.
B 7-8 per ring Strong. Well-developed with clear detail but may lack validation against real customer conversations or miss some dimensions.
C 5-6 per ring Adequate. Present and directionally correct but based more on assumptions than research. Needs enrichment.
D/F 1-4 per ring Thin or absent. Mentioned in passing but not developed as a persona. Cannot inform decisions.

Section 06

Product vs. Marketing Persona Alignment

Three persona layers exist independently. Here's where they agree, where they diverge, and what to do about it.

The Three Layers

Layer Location Purpose Personas Included
Product PRDs Confluence SD + PROD spaces Engineering specs: epics, user stories, permissions, APIs Librarian, Superintendent, Teacher, Guardian, SuperAdmin
Marketing Personas Confluence TS1 + SH spaces Sales/marketing: messaging, value props, conversation frameworks Superintendent, Librarian, CFO, Board, Parent, ESC, Publisher, Investor
Psychographics customer-psychographics.md (local) Operational: buying behavior, objections, content preferences, segments Superintendent, Librarian, Board Member, Curriculum Director, ESC Partner

Where They Align

  • Superintendent — consistent across all 3 layers. Core fear, buyer role, and messaging match.
  • Librarian — consistent across all 3 layers. Named persona "Paige Turner" used in PRDs, product terminology ("Compliance Gatekeeper") aligns with marketing positioning.
  • Board Member — marketing and psychographics align as "ratifier" role. No product PRD (correct — they don't use the product directly).

Where They Diverge

  • Teacher — full product PRD, zero marketing presence. Product team built for them; marketing team deprioritized them.
  • Parent — full product PRD, minimal marketing mention. OnShelf Engage is built but its users aren't understood from a marketing perspective.
  • Curriculum Director — full psychographic profile in codebase, explicitly deprioritized in Confluence marketing. Needs reconciliation.
  • CFO — present in Confluence marketing, absent from psychographics file and PRDs. Needs decision: stakeholder profile or full persona?
  • ESC Partner — full psychographic + Confluence marketing, but no product PRD. Correct — they're channel partners, not users.

The Structural Recommendation

The three layers serve different purposes and should contain different personas. But they need a cross-reference map and a single index that answers: "What do we know about [persona], and where does it live?" The Personas & PRDs Index page (56131585) does this for product but doesn't link to marketing or psychographic sources. customer-psychographics.md references Confluence pages as data sources but doesn't link to PRDs. A unified persona index — even just a table — would close this gap.

Section 07

Prioritized Recommendations

Ranked by impact on Bookmarked's ability to sell, onboard, and retain districts.

1

Ship the "For Librarians" Website Page

Quick Win

The messaging is written (Confluence 71204893), the HubSpot page exists (173284618778), and it's in draft. This is the highest-ROI persona action because librarians are your user-champions who drive adoption from below. Publishing this page means librarians searching for compliance solutions find Bookmarked speaking directly to them in the "Practical Partner" voice, not just the superintendent-facing liability messaging.

Effort: Low (content exists, page exists in HubSpot). Impact: High (librarian organic discovery + internal advocacy ammunition).

2

Build a Parent/Guardian Marketing Persona

Critical Gap

OnShelf Engage is a differentiated product ("transforms adversarial to collaborative") with a full PRD but zero understanding of the parent as a marketing audience. Build a persona that answers: Who is the parent who engages? (Not all will.) What's their motivation — is it concern about content, interest in their child's reading, or something else? What triggers activation after they receive the invite email? What does success look like to them? This persona directly informs: (a) the invite email copy, (b) the onboarding flow messaging, (c) whether OnShelf Engage warrants its own website section.

Effort: Medium (needs research — Plaud transcripts may have parent mentions). Impact: High (OnShelf Engage adoption is a retention lever and a sales differentiator).

3

Add HubSpot Persona Property

Operational Blocker

Create a custom contact property bookmarked_persona (dropdown: Superintendent, Librarian, Board Member, Curriculum Director, ESC Partner, Parent, Teacher, CFO, IT Director, Other) and a custom property buying_segment (dropdown: Proactive Protector, Crisis Responder, Innovation Leader, Peer Follower, Unknown). Retroactively tag existing contacts using job title mapping. This unlocks: persona-based email campaigns, persona-filtered reporting, persona-specific lead scoring, and ROI tracking by persona. Without this, all your persona work stays theoretical.

Effort: Low-Medium (property creation is simple; backfill requires a mapping script). Impact: High (operationalizes the entire persona framework).

4

Build a Teacher Adoption Persona

Not a buyer persona — an adoption persona. Teachers don't buy Bookmarked, but they must adopt the Classroom Library Module for it to deliver district-wide value. This persona should answer: What motivates a teacher to catalog their classroom library? What are their barriers (time, confusion, "another system")? What does the onboarding journey look like? This directly informs in-app messaging, admin rollout guides, and the value narrative for districts considering the Classroom Library Module.

Effort: Medium. Impact: Medium-High (Classroom Library visibility is a differentiator districts want but only works with teacher adoption).

5

Create a Unified Persona Index

A single Confluence page (or local file) that serves as the master cross-reference: for each persona, link to the product PRD, the marketing persona page, the psychographics section, and note the website presence. This eliminates the "which source do I trust?" confusion and makes persona governance auditable. Model it on the existing Personas & PRDs Index (56131585) but expand it to include marketing sources.

Effort: Low. Impact: Medium (structural clarity, prevents drift).

6

Reconcile Curriculum Director Status

Decide: Is the Curriculum Director an active persona or not? customer-psychographics.md says yes (full Profile #4). Confluence marketing says "deprioritized." If active, update Confluence. If deprioritized, remove from psychographics and note the reasoning (e.g., "curriculum directors are reached through superintendent, not directly"). Either answer is fine — the ambiguity is the problem.

Effort: Low (decision, not research). Impact: Medium (eliminates a governance inconsistency).

7

Add Day-in-the-Life Narratives to Top 2 Personas

The Superintendent and Librarian personas are strong on psychographics but could benefit from a narrative format: "It's 7:30am on a Tuesday, and Superintendent Johnson opens an email from the ESC about new TEA guidance..." This format makes personas usable for content creation (blog posts, case studies, video scripts) and makes them sticky for the team. Revella calls these "buying insight stories" — they bring the persona to life beyond bullet points.

Effort: Low-Medium. Impact: Medium (content enablement).

8

Name the Superintendent Persona

The librarian has "Paige Turner" — a memorable composite name that makes the persona tangible. The superintendent doesn't. Give them a name (following the "Paige Turner" naming convention, use something relevant like "Mark Canton" or similar). Named personas are referenced more frequently by teams and create a shared language for decision-making ("Would Mark buy this?" vs. "Would a superintendent buy this?").

Effort: Trivial. Impact: Low-Medium (team adoption of persona-driven thinking).
