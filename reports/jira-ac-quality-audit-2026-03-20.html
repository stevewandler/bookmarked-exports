Executive Summary

99 tickets were audited across 11 sprints (Sprint 55–65) plus unassigned backlog. Each ticket was evaluated against a single question: Can an AI agent (e.g., Playwright) translate these acceptance criteria directly into test assertions without human interpretation?

Overall, 74 of 99 tickets (75%) have machine-testable acceptance criteria. The remaining 25 tickets (25%) either lack AC entirely, have requirements-only language, or have AC too vague for automated testing.

The trend is clearly improving: Sprint 55 scored 25%, while Sprints 61–64 are averaging 95%+. However, two systemic issues persist across all sprints:

  • 0% of tickets have an assignee — every single ticket shows "Unassigned"
  • Only 9% have story points estimated — capacity planning is impossible

Key Findings

AC quality is trending up: Sprint 55 scored 25%. By Sprint 60+, the pass rate jumps to 80-100%. The March 15 batch of Orders Phase 1 stories (BOOK-893 through BOOK-911) all have solid AC with verifiable assertions. The March 19 bug tickets (BOOK-913 through BOOK-921) are particularly well-written with steps to reproduce, expected/actual behavior, and clear AC.
Critical gap — Zero assignees: Not a single ticket in the last 4 weeks has an assignee. This means there is no accountability trail, no way to measure individual throughput, and no way to balance workload across the team. This is the single most urgent process fix needed.
Story points nearly absent: Only 9 tickets out of 99 have story point estimates (all from Sprint 62–63). Without estimates, sprint commitment is guesswork, velocity cannot be measured, and capacity planning is impossible.
18 tickets have NO acceptance criteria section at all: These are mostly older bugs from Sprint 55–57 that have steps to reproduce but no explicit pass/fail criteria. While STR is good (94% of bugs have it), the absence of AC means there is no definition of "fixed." Three more recent unassigned bugs (BOOK-877, 878, 879) also lack AC.

Methodology

Each ticket's description was programmatically analyzed for:
1. Presence of AC section — Does the description contain an "Acceptance Criteria" heading?
2. Observable assertions — Does the AC contain verifiable outcomes (e.g., "X is displayed," "API returns 200," "user sees Y," "clicking A shows B")?
3. GIVEN/WHEN/THEN format — Does the AC use structured behavioral test format?
4. Requirements vs. test language — Does the AC describe what to build ("add field X to schema") vs. how to verify it works ("when user opens page, field X shows value")?

A ticket passes if it has at least 2 checklist items with verifiable assertions, or 3+ observable outcome patterns, or uses GIVEN/WHEN/THEN format.
A ticket fails if it has no AC section, only requirements language, or insufficient observable outcomes.

Scope: 99 tickets from the BOOK project updated between Feb 20 and Mar 20, 2026. Epics and [REMOVED] tickets were excluded. Bugs were additionally evaluated for steps-to-reproduce.
Sprints covered: Sprint 55 through Sprint 65, plus 32 tickets in backlog (no sprint assigned).
